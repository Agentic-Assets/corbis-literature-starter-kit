# Journal Targets for Real Estate Research

Use these notes as a practical targeting aid, not as a substitute for checking the latest submission page.

## Primary real estate journals

### Real Estate Economics (REE)
- Flagship journal of AREUEA. Highest prestige among dedicated real estate journals.
- Abstract: 100 words, italicized, readable by a layperson, independent of the paper.
- Block format, double spacing. Do not number sections.
- Keep the introduction brief.
- Tables and figures self-explanatory, on separate pages.
- Regression tables: coefficients and t-statistics in separate columns preferred.
- Values: institutional knowledge, clean identification, policy relevance, and spatial awareness.
- Typical paper: well-identified empirical design exploiting real-estate-specific institutional features.

### Journal of Real Estate Finance and Economics (JREFE)
- Abstract: 150-250 words with 4-6 keywords.
- Author-year citations; alphabetized reference list. DOI links.
- No more than three heading levels. Footnotes rather than endnotes.
- More receptive to methodological detail, longer papers, and niche topics than REE.
- Good for papers with strong institutional knowledge but narrower appeal.

## Cross-over urban/housing journals

### Journal of Urban Economics (JUE)
- Urban economics framing preferred: housing, land use, transportation, local public finance, agglomeration.
- Values structural approaches and general equilibrium reasoning more than pure real estate journals.
- Good for papers that connect real estate outcomes to broader urban economics questions.
- Abstract up to 100 words.

### Journal of Housing Economics (JHE)
- Housing-focused: prices, affordability, policy, mortgage markets, household mobility.
- More receptive to descriptive and policy-oriented work than JUE or REE.
- Moderate length abstracts. Good for applied policy analysis.

## Finance journals that publish real estate

### JF, JFE, RFS
- Publish real estate papers when the question has broad finance relevance (e.g., mortgage markets and financial stability, housing and household portfolios, REIT pricing).
- Must frame as a finance contribution, not a real estate contribution.
- Abstract: 100 words. See finance track standards.

### JFQA, Management Science
- Occasionally publish housing/mortgage finance papers.
- Frame around the finance or management question, not the real estate setting.

## Choosing between tracks

**Choose real estate track when**: The paper's contribution depends on institutional detail (zoning, appraisal, local policy), spatial structure, or property-market-specific mechanisms that a finance generalist would not fully appreciate.

**Choose finance track when**: The real estate setting provides clean identification for a broad finance question (e.g., information asymmetry in lending, household risk management, collateral and credit cycles).

**Cross-track papers**: Mortgage finance, REIT pricing, household portfolio choice, and housing wealth effects can target either track. Consider where the strongest referees are and where the contribution would be best appreciated.

## Practical writing takeaways
- Real estate journals reward institutional depth, spatial awareness, and policy relevance.
- Maps, institutional timelines, and local-market context should do real analytical work.
- Explain the unit of analysis clearly: property-level, borrower-level, neighborhood-level, or market-level.
- Address spatial dependence in standard errors explicitly — this is expected.
- Do not overclaim novelty. Use "we contribute to" or "we provide evidence on."
